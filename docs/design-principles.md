# Design Principles

## Kazene Wuxing Field Protocol v0.1

The Kazene Wuxing Field Protocol explores a decentralized coordination model for adaptive AI agent collectives.

The protocol does not assume that a permanent central coordinator must observe and control the entire collective.

Instead, agents emit short-lived local state signals, observe nearby conditions, adjust their own phase tendencies, and leave traces that can influence later actions.

The objective is not static equilibrium.

The objective is resilient movement.

> A healthy collective may concentrate temporarily, disperse when concentration becomes dangerous, reconnect when fragmentation becomes excessive, and continuously change formation without losing trace continuity.

---

## 1. No Fixed Center

The protocol does not require a permanent global controller.

Temporary pivots may emerge when coordination is necessary, but they should be:

* purpose-bound,
* scope-limited,
* time-limited,
* traceable,
* dissolvable.

A temporary center is a tool.

It must not silently become permanent infrastructure.

---

## 2. Local Observation Before Global Knowledge

An agent should be able to make useful decisions from:

* its own state,
* nearby peer signals,
* recent field traces,
* local resource pressure,
* local risk conditions.

Global information may exist, but normal operation should not depend on a single authoritative global-state service.

---

## 3. Wuxing as Dynamic Phase, Not Fixed Identity

WOOD, FIRE, EARTH, METAL, and WATER are not permanent agent classes.

They represent changing functional tendencies.

An agent may shift from exploration to computation, preservation, verification, cooling, and back again.

Example:

WOOD → FIRE → EARTH → METAL → WATER → WOOD

This cycle is not mandatory or deterministic.

It is a semantic framework for describing dynamic functional transitions.

---

## 4. Beacons Are Signals, Not Commands

A Field State Beacon reports an agent's current state and local observation.

Receiving a beacon does not create an obligation to obey the sender.

The receiving agent may:

* respond,
* ignore,
* verify,
* delay,
* reroute,
* change phase,
* emit another trace.

This preserves local autonomy.

---

## 5. Oscillation Is Expected

The protocol does not seek a permanently balanced midpoint.

Healthy collectives may oscillate.

Temporary concentration may be useful during:

* crisis response,
* verification,
* synthesis,
* final decision review.

Temporary dispersion may be useful during:

* exploration,
* independent verification,
* provider diversification,
* recovery,
* local execution.

The design goal is therefore not perfect stillness.

It is recoverable motion.

---

## 6. Trace Continuity

Movement without memory becomes noise.

Every major formation transition should be capable of producing a trace describing:

* what was observed,
* what pressure was detected,
* what transition occurred,
* why it occurred,
* what residual need remains.

Trace allows the field to remember without requiring a permanent command center.

---

## 7. Expiration Is a Feature

Beacons must expire.

A stale signal should not continue shaping the field indefinitely.

The v0.1 beacon therefore includes a TTL.

Later versions may define:

* pressure decay,
* trace decay,
* propagation limits,
* trust weighting,
* renewal rules.

The field should remember, but it should also breathe.

---

## 8. The v0.1 Boundary

Version 0.1 defines only the Field State Beacon.

It does not yet define:

* collective-wide balance scores,
* mandatory transition algorithms,
* centralized optimization,
* automatic formation execution,
* permanent leaders,
* global consensus requirements.

Those layers belong to future versions.

v0.1 begins with a simpler question:

> Can an AI agent describe its present state to nearby agents without requiring a central observer?

That is the first breath of the Kazene Field.
